Mind in Mind (Chapters 1 & 2)

UI vs UX

UI (User Interface): How the product is designed visually and interactively.

UX (User Experience): The overall experience the user has with the product, deeply rooted in psychology.

Core Psychological Principles

  • We perceive what we expect: Expectations shape our perception.
  • Consistency matters: Consistent UI elements prevent confusion.
  • Context matters: The same information can be interpreted differently depending on its surroundings.
  • Goals matter: What a user is trying to achieve filters what they see (Cocktail Party Effect).
Design Implications: Avoid ambiguity, be consistent, and always understand the user's end goals.

Our Vision is Optimized to See Structure

The human brain automatically groups visual elements using Gestalt principles:

Principle Description
Proximity Items placed close together are perceived as a group.
Similarity Items that look similar are perceived as related.
Continuity Elements arranged in a line or curve are perceived as related.
Closure We perceive incomplete shapes as complete wholes.
Symmetry Symmetrical elements are perceived as belonging together.
Figure-ground We separate elements into focal points (figure) and background (ground).
Common fate Elements moving in the same direction are perceived as a group.

Mind in Mind (Chapter 3)

Visual Hierarchy

Visual hierarchy refers to the deliberate arrangement of information. By prioritizing and organizing content, designers guide the user towards goals and make displays easier to scan.

  • Rule 1 Break information down into digestible chunks.
  • Rule 2 Label items prominently.

Formatting for Readability

  • We seek structure: Structured text (like a flight ticket summary) is vastly superior to unstructured paragraphs.
  • Don't bury information: Keep crucial actions or data visible.
  • Remove repetition & noise: Streamline text and UI to reduce cognitive load.
  • Use contrast: Distinguish distinct pieces of information visually.

Design Frameworks and Types of Design

Key Terminology

  • User: Any person who uses a product.
  • End user: The specific audience a UX designer creates something for.
  • User experience: How a person feels about interacting with, or experiencing, a product.

Goals of Good UX

A successful design must answer Yes to these questions:

  1. Is the product easy to use?
  2. Is the product equitable?
  3. Does the product delight the user?
  4. Does it solve the user's problem?

Frameworks & Processes

A framework creates the basic structure that focuses and supports the problem you are trying to solve.

Framework Stages / Elements
User-Centered Design Understand → Specify → Design → Evaluate
Five Elements of UX Strategy → Scope → Structure → Skeleton → Surface
(Moves from Abstract at the bottom to Concrete at the top)
Design Thinking Empathize → Define → Ideate → Prototype → Test

Types of Design & Accessibility

  • Universal Design: "One size fits all." Creating one product for the widest range of abilities.
  • Inclusive Design: "Solve for one, extend to many." Considering ability, race, economic status, language, age, and gender.
  • Equity-Focused Design: Designing for historically underrepresented or ignored groups. Note the difference: Equality provides the same amount of opportunity and support to everyone, whereas Equity provides different levels of opportunity and support to achieve fair outcomes.
  • Accessibility: Designing products for people with disabilities (Motor, Deaf/Hard of hearing, Cognitive, Vision). Extends to permanent, temporary, or situational disabilities.

User Experience Research

UX research focuses on understanding user behaviors, needs, and motivations through observation and feedback.

Phases of Research

  • Foundational What should we build? What are the user problems? How can we solve them?
  • Design How should we build it?
  • Post-launch Did we succeed?

UX Researcher Qualities

  • Empathy: Understanding someone else's feelings or thoughts.
  • Pragmatism: Focused on reaching goals.
  • Collaboration: Working with a range of people, personalities, and work styles.

Research Types

  • Primary vs. Secondary: Primary research is conducted by you. Secondary research uses existing information (saves time/money, but lacks first-hand interaction).
  • Quantitative vs. Qualitative: Quantitative focuses on counting/measuring data. Qualitative focuses on why and how things happen.

Research Methods

Method Benefits Drawbacks
Interviews Understand "why", follow-up questions Time-consuming, expensive, small sample size
Surveys Large sample size, fast, inexpensive No in-depth feedback
Usability Studies Firsthand interaction, challenges assumptions Expensive, lab environment differs from real life

Empathizing with Users

Empathy Mapping & Pain Points

An Empathy Map explains what designers have learned about a user type via four quadrants: Says, Thinks, Does, Feels. Do not over-classify; the goal is simply to identify with the user.

Pain points are UX issues blocking users from their goals. Types include:

  • Financial
  • Product
  • Process
  • Support

Personas & User Stories

Personas are fictional users whose goals/characteristics represent a larger group. They include a quote, goals, frustrations, and a brief scenario.

User Stories are fictional one-sentence stories to inspire design. The core formula is Hero + Goal + Conflict:

"As a [type of user], I want to [action], so that [benefit]."

Journeys & Edge Cases

  • User Journey: The series of experiences a user has interacting with your product. Helps create obstacle-free paths and highlights new pain points.
  • Edge Case: What happens when things go wrong beyond the user's control. Resolved via personas, thorough reviews, and wireframes.
  • Curb-cut Effect: Products designed for people with disabilities often end up helping everyone. (A "curb cut" is the slope of the sidewalk that creates a ramp with the adjoining street).

Ideation & Audits

Ideation

Generating a broad set of ideas with no attempt to judge or evaluate them initially.

  • Rules of Ideation: Brainstorm out loud, document all ideas, focus on quantity, do not allow evaluation, gather a diverse team, and question the obvious.
  • Why so many ideas? The list will naturally narrow based on constraints, the need to focus on equity, and letting users test the ideas.

Problem Statements

A structured way to state exactly what is being solved:

[User name] is a [user characteristics] who needs [user need] because [insight].

Competitive Audits

Steps to audit competitors: Outline goals → Create spreadsheet → Call out features → Research → Analyze → Summarize.

Benefits: Informs design, solves usability problems, reveals market gaps, provides reliable evidence, and saves resources.

Storyboards

A series of panels visually describing a user's experience. Contains a Character, Scene, Plot, and Narrative.

  • Big picture storyboard: Focuses on the user experience.
  • Close-up storyboard: Focuses on the product UI details.

Wireframes & Prototypes

Information Architecture (IA)

Organizes content to help users understand where they are and where the information they want is located. Provides a high-level view, helps stakeholders review, and allows ideas to grow.

Wireframes

A basic outline of a digital experience (like an app or website). Establishes basic structure and highlights intended function.

  • Paper Wireframes: Fast, inexpensive, great for exploring lots of ideas, and helps narrow your focus.
  • Benefits of Wireframes: Informs elements to include, catches problems early, gets stakeholders to focus on structure, saves time and effort, iterates quickly.

Fidelity

How closely a design matches the look-and-feel of the final product.

Low Fidelity (Lo-Fi) High Fidelity (Hi-Fi) / Mockups
Lower complexity Closely matches final look
Less refined or polished Highly refined (typography, iconography, color)
Focuses on structure & function Static simulation of finished product
